# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Inkwell is designed primarily for one serious personal reader and note taker
who researches topics through articles and PDFs. The user moves between a
desktop browser, iPhone, and iPad, saving material when it appears and returning
later for focused reading, annotation, and synthesis.

The primary job is to build understanding without losing context: capture useful
material quickly, read it without distraction, mark important passages, attach
notes or voice memos, and trust that the full research trail follows across
devices.

## Product Purpose

Inkwell is a calm personal research environment for collecting, reading, and
annotating long-form material. It should reduce the friction between discovering
something worth reading and turning it into durable understanding.

Success means the interface recedes during reading, annotations feel naturally
attached to the source, and moving between devices never breaks concentration.
The product should make a growing research library feel legible and intentional,
not like an inbox demanding attention.

## Brand Personality

Calm, serious, considered.

Inkwell should feel composed and trustworthy, with the quiet confidence of
Apple Books. Its voice is concise, mature, and direct. It can have warmth and
material character, but never at the expense of clarity or concentration.

## Anti-references

Inkwell should not resemble a children's journal, scrapbook, diary, or
gamified note-taking app. Avoid playful stationery motifs, stickers, novelty
illustrations, bubbly controls, bright rainbow palettes, exaggerated handwriting
effects, celebratory animation, and decorative clutter.

It should also avoid generic productivity-dashboard styling that turns reading
into a collection of metrics, cards, badges, and activity prompts.

## Design Principles

1. Protect the reading state. Content and comprehension take priority over
   controls, navigation, and product decoration.
2. Make research continuity invisible. Saving, syncing, resuming, and moving
   between devices should feel like one uninterrupted workspace.
3. Prefer quiet confidence over novelty. Use familiar, precise interaction
   patterns and reserve personality for details that deepen the reading
   experience.
4. Treat annotations as part of the source. Notes, marks, outlines, and voice
   memos should remain spatially and conceptually connected to the material.
5. Let the library clarify, not pressure. Organization should help the user
   return to meaningful work without introducing inbox anxiety or gamification.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target strong contrast and support platform accessibility conventions,
including Dynamic Type where practical, VoiceOver, keyboard navigation on web,
visible focus states, and reduced-motion preferences. Do not rely on color alone
to communicate state.
